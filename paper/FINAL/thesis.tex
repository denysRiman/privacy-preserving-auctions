\begin{kurzfassung}
Diese Arbeit untersucht, wie wirtschaftlich sichere Zwei-Parteien-Berechnung
(2PC) auf Ethereum für private Gebotsprotokolle umgesetzt werden kann. Der
Ausgangspunkt ist ein zentrales Spannungsfeld öffentlicher Blockchains:
Transparenz erhöht die Nachvollziehbarkeit, begünstigt aber zugleich
strategisches Verhalten durch sichtbare Transaktionen und Reihenfolgeeffekte.
Für versiegelte Auktionen führt dies zu einer direkten Herausforderung für
Fairness, Vertraulichkeit und Durchsetzbarkeit.

Die Forschungsfrage lautet, wie ein Protokoll gestaltet werden kann, das
gleichzeitig (i) Eingabeprivatheit der Gebote, (ii) öffentlich überprüfbare
Durchsetzung, (iii) Robustheit gegenüber Verzögerung und Abbruch sowie
(iv) praktische Umsetzbarkeit unter Ethereum-Randbedingungen erreicht.
Methodisch kombiniert die Arbeit ein Off-Chain-2PC-Design auf Basis von
Garbled Circuits und Oblivious Transfer mit einer On-Chain-Statusmaschine.
Der Smart Contract erzwingt Phasenübergänge, Commitments, Deadlines,
Dispute-Pfade und deterministische Settlement-Regeln.

Als Ergebnis wurde ein Proof-of-Concept in Solidity und Rust implementiert und
in End-to-End-Szenarien evaluiert (ehrlicher Ablauf, Evaluator-Default,
Garbler-Cheating). Die Evaluation zeigt, dass
Anchor-gebundenes Settlement (\texttt{hOut}), begrenzte aber öffentliche
Verifizierbarkeit über explizite Dispute-Prädikate sowie Liveness durch
Default-/Slashing-Mechanismen in einer konsistenten Ausführungslogik kombiniert
werden können. Damit wird gezeigt, dass ökonomisch sichere 2PC auf Ethereum im
Protokollkern praktisch realisierbar ist. Als Anwendungsschicht wird eine
ENS-Auktionsabbildung demonstriert. Die Arbeit grenzt zugleich klar ab, dass
vollständige Gas-Kalibrierung unter Produktionsbedingungen und weitergehende
ökonomische Parametersynthese zukünftige Arbeiten sind.
\end{kurzfassung}

\begin{abstract}
In this thesis, we examine how economically secure two-party computation (2PC)
can be realized on Ethereum for private bidding protocols. The motivating
tension is structural: public-chain transparency supports auditability, yet the
same visibility exposes transaction data and ordering opportunities that can
distort fairness and correctness in sealed-bid execution.

Our central question is whether one protocol can preserve bid privacy until
valid settlement, remain publicly enforceable, tolerate strategic delay or
abort behavior, and still be practically deployable under Ethereum constraints.
To address this, we combine off-chain garbled-circuit/OT execution with an
on-chain enforcement state machine. The contract governs phase transitions,
commitments, deadlines, dispute entry points, and deterministic settlement
rules.

Our implementation delivers a Solidity+Rust proof of concept, evaluated in
end-to-end scenarios: an honest run, an evaluator-defaulted liveness path, and
a garbler-cheat dispute path. We observe that structured output-anchor binding
(\texttt{hOut}), selective publicly verifiable dispute predicates, and
deadline-based default/slashing logic can be integrated into a single coherent
protocol flow. This supports the practical feasibility of economically secure
2PC on Ethereum at the protocol-core layer. We further implement an ENS auction
mapping as an application layer over the same enforcement backbone.

We also state clear boundaries: full production-grade gas calibration and
deeper economic parameter optimization remain future work.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Context and Motivation}
Public blockchains provide strong auditability, but that property comes with a direct tradeoff: transaction data and transaction ordering are visible before final settlement. On Ethereum, this transparency enables adversarial strategies such as reordering, insertion, and censorship, often discussed under maximal extractable value (MEV)~\cite{Wood2025Ethereum,Daian2019FlashBoys}. For protocols with private inputs, this visibility is not a side issue; it directly affects fairness and correctness.

Sealed-bid auctions make this tension concrete. Bids should remain private during execution, while the final winner and settlement must still be publicly enforceable. If private information leaks too early, strategic behavior can distort the outcome and reduce trust in the mechanism. If enforcement is weak, parties can deviate or abort at points where deviation is economically beneficial.

Recent work by Alexopoulos et al. (BitPriv) shows that privacy-preserving and economically enforceable computation can be achieved in a Bitcoin-native environment through off-chain two-party computation (2PC), collateral-backed incentives, and on-chain dispute resolution~\cite{BitPriv2025}. Building on that direction, this thesis addresses Ethereum directly and develops a protocol and prototype tailored to Ethereum execution constraints, with explicit state-machine enforcement, scoped disputes, and deterministic settlement rules.

\section{Problem Statement}
The central challenge is to design a protocol for sealed-bid auctions on Ethereum that satisfies four properties simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Input privacy}: bids are not revealed before the protocol reaches a valid settlement point.
\item \textbf{Public enforceability}: the on-chain contract can enforce the agreed output and financial transfers.
\item \textbf{Robustness to strategic behavior}: the protocol remains operational when parties stall, abort, or provide inconsistent artifacts.
\item \textbf{Practical deployability}: on-chain overhead remains manageable and implementation complexity stays within realistic engineering bounds.
\end{enumerate}

In public-blockchain deployments, these goals interact in non-trivial ways. Stronger cryptographic guarantees may increase operational cost, while cheaper constructions can weaken deterrence against rational deviations. The design therefore has to be reasoned about not only cryptographically, but also economically: the expected payoff of honest behavior should dominate profitable deviation under explicit assumptions~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC}.

Accordingly, this thesis follows an \emph{optimistic, dispute-driven enforcement} model.
On-chain logic enforces deadlines, deposits, slashing, settlement-anchor checks,
and selective dispute predicates, where dispute handling includes bounded,
selective on-chain GC/OT checks via partial recomputation/verification of
challenged steps. Off-chain logic performs the full GC/OT evaluation and
derives \texttt{outputBytes}. In this split, on-chain logic enforces binding,
deadlines, and accountable state transitions, while computational integrity is
established through deterministic off-chain execution and dispute evidence.

\section{Research Objectives}
Following the approved thesis proposal, the objective is to design and prototype an economically secure 2PC protocol for Ethereum-based sealed-bid auctions, with first-price auctions as the baseline use case.

Concretely, the thesis targets:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a protocol architecture that combines off-chain 2PC execution with on-chain commitments, deadlines, and disputes;
\item a threat and assumptions model suitable for adversarial transaction environments;
\item a Solidity + Rust proof-of-concept demonstrating end-to-end execution;
\item an evaluation of feasibility, including cost/performance characteristics and dispute-path practicality;
\item an economic-security analysis that explains how deposits, collateral, and penalties deter rational cheating.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions of This Thesis}
The expected contributions are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Ethereum-oriented protocol design:} a protocol that preserves bid privacy while keeping settlement publicly enforceable in a hostile execution environment.
\item \textbf{Economic-security framing:} a practical incentive model for rational participants, including explicit assumptions and deterrence conditions.
\item \textbf{Prototype implementation:} an integrated proof-of-concept with on-chain contract logic and off-chain 2PC components.
\item \textbf{Empirical evidence:} implementation-driven measurements and scenario-based analysis of optimistic and dispute executions.
\item \textbf{Ethereum-specific protocol engineering:} a concrete protocol realization for Ethereum with explicit phase transitions, enforceable dispute predicates, and settlement logic.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\section{Secure Two-Party Computation Basics}
Secure two-party computation (2PC) addresses a basic but demanding question: how can two mutually distrustful parties evaluate a function over private inputs while revealing only the prescribed output? In this thesis, the technical baseline follows the garbled-circuit line of work: one party garbles the circuit, and the other evaluates it using input labels obtained via oblivious transfer (OT). Foundational properties, formal treatment, and malicious-security efficiency tradeoffs are established in the classical and modern literature~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.

For blockchain applications, this model is attractive because the cryptographic heavy lifting can stay off-chain, while on-chain logic focuses on commitments, deadlines, and settlement enforcement. The split directly supports the practical objective of lowering gas costs without giving up privacy or accountability.

\section{From Semi-Honest to Malicious Security}
Under the semi-honest model, parties follow the protocol but still attempt to extract extra information from observed transcripts. For financial workflows, that assumption is typically too weak, since strategic deviation is economically incentivized. Moving to malicious security adds explicit defenses such as cut-and-choose checks for garbling correctness, commitment-consistency checks, and controlled abort handling~\cite{LindellPinkas2008}.

The engineering tradeoff is straightforward: stronger malicious-security guarantees usually increase both communication and verification overhead. In public-blockchain deployments, those costs map directly to on-chain expense and operational burden, so protocol design must continuously balance cryptographic rigor against deployability.

\section{Public Verifiability and Economic Enforcement}
Publicly verifiable 2PC seeks to make violations externally checkable, so a third party or smart contract can arbitrate disputes without replaying the entire off-chain protocol. Zhu et al.\ provide an influential direction, integrating dispute resolution with blockchain-based enforcement in financially secure computation settings~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC}.

BitPriv develops this line further by combining off-chain 2PC with collateral-backed on-chain punishment and a publicly verifiable dispute process in a Bitcoin-compatible framework~\cite{BitPriv2025}. This thesis formalizes that \emph{economic-security} perspective for Ethereum: participants are modeled as rational agents who may deviate when profitable, so deposits, slashing rules, and deadlines must be calibrated such that honest execution is the dominant economic strategy.

\section{Adversarial Execution on Public Blockchains}
Ethereum is transparent by construction. Mempool visibility and transaction-ordering freedom create systematic room for adversarial behavior, commonly analyzed through MEV-related strategies~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}. Auction-like protocols are especially exposed, since even partial leaks can influence bidding behavior and final settlement outcomes.

At the same time, Ethereum offers the enforcement substrate needed for commitment checks, dispute escalation, and deterministic settlement rules through publicly verifiable state and programmable contracts. The design problem is therefore twofold: hide sensitive inputs while retaining enforceable and cost-bounded execution under adversarial ordering~\cite{Wood2025Ethereum}.

\section{Ethereum and the EVM}
\subsection{Ethereum as Settlement Layer}
A blockchain can be modeled as a replicated append-only state machine
maintained under decentralized consensus.
Ethereum extends this model with programmable smart contracts, enabling
stateful protocol logic without a trusted intermediary~\cite{Wood2025Ethereum}.
For this thesis, a suitable settlement layer must satisfy four practical
conditions: executable enforcement logic, native asset custody, publicly
verifiable state transitions, and interoperability with existing on-chain
applications.
Ethereum is relevant because it meets these conditions in a mature deployment
environment.

Beyond those baseline conditions, four deployment properties dominate protocol
design. Ecosystem maturity matters because protocol execution depends on
surrounding infrastructure: account abstractions, wallets, token standards,
indexing tools, and operational monitoring all shape practical deployability.
Composability matters because smart contracts can interact with existing assets
and application modules through shared state and deterministic calls, so
enforcement logic can plug into broader market workflows rather than a closed
system. Economic security matters because settlement semantics rely on
economically secured consensus and transaction inclusion, not only local
contract logic. Public accountability matters because transparent contract
state and execution traces make commitment checks, timeout transitions, dispute
outcomes, and payouts externally auditable.

These properties are precisely the features needed by an economically secure
private protocol: private computation off-chain, but objectively auditable
enforcement on-chain.
The same environment also imposes strict cost and transparency constraints that
shape protocol architecture.

\subsection{EVM Constraints and Architectural Implications}
Ethereum contract logic is executed in the Ethereum Virtual Machine (EVM), a
deterministic bytecode runtime replicated across validators.
Deterministic replicated execution is essential for public enforceability:
every validator must reach the same post-state from the same input
transaction.
This makes the EVM a strong substrate for escrow, collateral locking, hash
checks, deadline enforcement, and settlement transitions.

At the same time, EVM execution is explicitly resource-priced through gas.
Storage writes are expensive relative to transient computation, and both
data-heavy and loop-heavy verification paths scale poorly in cost. As a
result, large cryptographic artifacts and full transcript re-execution are not
practical default on-chain behavior. The EVM also provides no private memory
model for contract logic: calldata, storage, and state transitions are public,
which rules out confidential in-contract evaluation as a baseline design.

These constraints motivate the core architectural decision adopted in this
thesis:
\begin{enumerate}
\item execute heavy private computation off-chain,
\item keep on-chain logic focused on commitments, deadlines, and bounded
predicate checks,
\item use selective dispute predicates for public accountability rather than
universal on-chain recomputation.
\end{enumerate}
This is a deployment-oriented adaptation to EVM economics, not a reduction in
security intent.

\section{ENS Auctions}
The Ethereum Name Service (ENS) maps human-readable names (for example,
\texttt{alice.eth}) to Ethereum addresses and related records~\cite{EIP137ENS,ENSDocs}.
ENS names can carry direct utility as routing identifiers, but also identity and
branding premiums.
Memorable or category-defining names may therefore accumulate resale value,
which turns them into economically contested scarce assets.
This scarcity makes market-based allocation mechanisms, including auctions,
a natural design choice.

In a transparent blockchain environment, fully public bidding leaks
willingness-to-pay and strategy information, which can distort competition and
create strategic disadvantages under adversarial ordering conditions~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}.
Bid privacy is therefore economically meaningful, not merely a cosmetic feature.
In particular, transparent auction flows are exposed to:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{valuation leakage:} visible bids reveal reservation-price signals
and strategy profiles,
\item \textbf{reactive bidding:} participants can condition behavior on observed
partial information rather than sealed valuations,
\item \textbf{coordination and collusion channels:} transparency can simplify
monitoring and enforcement of cartel-like strategies,
\item \textbf{ordering manipulation/front-running:} observable transactions can
be strategically reordered or preempted in adversarial execution
environments~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}.
\end{enumerate}

At the same time, name assignment and payment settlement must remain publicly
enforceable.
ENS auctions therefore capture both the privacy requirement (for bid formation)
and the enforceability requirement (for final allocation and payment).
For this reason, they provide a suitable benchmark for economically secure 2PC
on Ethereum and a concrete application context for the protocol developed in
the following chapters.

\section{Privacy-Preserving Auctions on Blockchains}
Private auction design on public ledgers has been explored from several
directions. FAST targets fair auctions using secret transaction
techniques~\cite{David2021FAST}. In parallel, systems work studies broader
private-computation layers for blockchain applications: gcVM proposes publicly
auditable MPC-compatible execution ideas for EVM-like
environments~\cite{Yanai2026gcVM}, while COTI V2 explores a
confidentiality-oriented layer-2 approach with additional system
assumptions~\cite{Haloani2024COTIv2}.

Taken together, these lines of work show that confidentiality and public
enforceability can coexist, but only under specific trust, complexity, and
cost tradeoffs. This thesis concentrates on an L1-oriented Ethereum path with
explicit economic incentives and dispute handling, and aims to minimize
additional trust assumptions beyond the base chain.

\section{Deployment Considerations for Ethereum}
A practical Ethereum protocol must account for both execution costs and data
availability pathways. When large off-chain artifacts (for example, evaluation
payloads derived from garbled-circuit flow) are referenced by on-chain
commitments, the protocol has to define how those artifacts are published and
how publication is cryptographically bound to settlement logic. EIP-4844 is
therefore directly relevant: blob transactions provide a high-volume data
publication path with cost characteristics distinct from
calldata~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.

Accordingly, design choices in this thesis are evaluated not only at the level
of security semantics, but also for concrete deployment feasibility under
Ethereum's transaction and fee model.

\section{Positioning of This Thesis}
Relative to prior work, this thesis is positioned at the intersection of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item garbled-circuit-based 2PC foundations~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008},
\item publicly verifiable and financially motivated enforcement models~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC,BitPriv2025},
\item adversarial blockchain execution analysis with MEV-aware reasoning~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys,Wood2025Ethereum},
\item and private auction protocol design for practical deployment~\cite{David2021FAST,Yanai2026gcVM,Haloani2024COTIv2,Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\end{enumerate}

The main distinction from existing systems is the target setting:
economically secure 2PC for sealed-bid auctions on Ethereum, paired with an
explicit implementation and evaluation path that keeps protocol assumptions
aligned with engineering reality.

\chapter{Model and Requirements}
\section{System Model and Roles}
To keep protocol and application semantics clearly separated, this chapter uses
an explicit layer vocabulary:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Protocol (2PC core):} pairwise GC/OT execution between one
garbler $G$ (private input $x$) and one evaluator $E$ (private input $y$), with
transcript commitments and selective dispute predicates~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.
\item \textbf{Contract enforcement layer:} on-chain state machine for deadlines,
deposits, slashing, and cost-limited verification checks. The contract is an
enforcement mechanism, not a cryptographic party.
\item \textbf{Application instantiation:} auction-specific aggregation logic
(``many evaluators'' in protocol terms; ``buyers'' in the ENS application),
winner decoding, settlement formatting, and ENS assignment that consume already
bound outputs.
\end{enumerate}

Throughout this chapter, terminology is fixed as follows:
``2PC protocol'' denotes one GC/OT execution between a garbler and one evaluator,
while ``$n$-buyer auction'' means application-layer composition of multiple such
runs (one per evaluator/buyer) plus deterministic aggregation.

At the protocol-core level, the target computation is deterministic:
\[
z=f(x,y).
\]
In the auction instantiation, multiple pairwise runs are orchestrated, and
their outputs are aggregated by application logic. That auction logic is not
part of the 2PC protocol; it is an application-layer consumer of the protocol's
bound output.

Protocol-level privacy is defined per pairwise run: the garbler should not
learn the evaluator's private input beyond what is implied by the agreed
output. In the instantiated multi-buyer workflow, buyers should likewise not
learn other buyers' bids beyond output-implied information. These claims assume
correct off-chain channel and artifact handling; operational leak vectors such
as debug logs, local storage exposure, or external telemetry remain outside the
cryptographic guarantee boundary.

\section{Contract-Orchestration State Model}
The following phases describe contract orchestration for the chosen
instantiation. They are enforcement and liveness stages, not cryptographic 2PC
primitives:
\begin{enumerate}
\item deposits,
\item evaluator-seed commit and reveal,
\item core commitments and evaluator-scoped OT-root commitments,
\item evaluator liveness/input finalization,
\item cut-and-choose opening,
\item dispute window,
\item label reveal and settlement submission,
\item assignment/final payout or timeout resolution.
\end{enumerate}

For each circuit instance $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$, the model includes commitment
objects such as seed commitments (`comSeed`), garbled-artifact roots
(`rootGC`), evaluation blob hashes (`blobHashGC`), evaluator-scoped OT roots
(`buyerRootOTCommitment`), and output anchors (`hOut`). The selected evaluation
instance is derived from finalized verifier-seed material, and output validity
is modeled as hash binding to the committed anchor for that instance.

The design objective is to keep high-volume cryptographic artifacts off-chain
during normal execution while preserving a scoped, externally verifiable
dispute path. This aligns with practical Ethereum deployment constraints and
data-publication tradeoffs (including blob-based publication
options)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.

\section{Adversary and Deviation Model}
The adversarial model combines:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{malicious protocol behavior} at the party level (invalid commitments, inconsistent openings, selective abort),
\item \textbf{rational economic behavior} (deviation is attempted when expected utility is positive),
\item and \textbf{adversarial transaction environment} on public Ethereum (observable mempool, reorderability, latency asymmetry)~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}.
\end{enumerate}

The adversary can delay, reorder, or strategically time its own transactions,
but it cannot break standard cryptographic assumptions. The contract and chain
consensus are assumed to follow Ethereum's underlying security model. In this
thesis, base-consensus denial-of-service and chain-level catastrophic failures
are out of scope.

Representative deviations considered in the model include:
\begin{enumerate}
\item submitting malformed commitments,
\item opening data inconsistent with previous commitments,
\item issuing false disputes to delay settlement,
\item withholding required messages near deadlines,
\item and attempting invalid output submission.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Assumptions}
\textbf{A1. Cryptographic assumptions.}
Hash functions used for commitments and anchors are collision-resistant, and
the underlying garbled-circuit/OT constructions provide their expected security
properties under standard
assumptions~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.

\textbf{A2. Deterministic reconstruction interface.}
Given agreed seeds and domain-separation inputs, honest verifiers reconstruct
the same per-instance artifacts and transcript commitments. This assumption is
required so that disputes are objective and reproducible.

\textbf{A3. Contract-enforceable timing.}
Deadlines are represented in contract state and interpreted under Ethereum block
progression (deposit, verifier-seed, commitment, evaluator-input, open,
dispute, labels, settle windows). A party that misses a required action before
its deadline is treated as non-cooperative and penalized by protocol rules.

\textbf{A4. Data availability during dispute windows.}
Data needed for a valid challenge is available to challengers in time, either
by direct exchange or by a publication path bound to on-chain references (for
example, blob-hash anchored payloads)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
For blob-linked publication paths, this availability assumption is scoped to the
dispute window. Blob references are treated as retrieval anchors for challenge
resolution (implementation reference: Appendix~\ref{app:a4}).

\textbf{A5. Constrained on-chain verification.}
On-chain verification is intentionally constrained to selective checks (for
example, leaf-level consistency checks), aligning verification cost with
Ethereum deployment constraints.

\textbf{A6. Instantiation assumption: ENS adapter correctness and authority.}
For the ENS application mapping, the adapter called by the contract is assumed
to be authorized to perform assignment for the offered names and to implement
\texttt{assign(namehash,to)} correctly (without hidden side paths). Detailed ENS
ownership/registration mechanics are treated as application-integration context,
not as a protocol-core security claim of this thesis.

\section{Security Requirements}
Under the above model, requirements are separated into:
\textbf{security claims for the 2PC core} (R1--R3) and
\textbf{engineering requirements for enforcement/instantiation} (R4--R6).

\textbf{R1. Input privacy.}
No party learns the counterparty's private input beyond what is implied by the
prescribed output and mandatory on-chain disclosures. This requirement follows
the BitPriv covert-2PC privacy objective and standard GC/OT security
assumptions~\cite{BitPriv2025,Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.

\textbf{R2. Output correctness and binding.}
An accepted settlement must correspond to a valid protocol-consistent output.
Invalid output labels or inconsistent evidence must be rejectable by the
contract. This requirement is aligned with BitPriv's enforceable-correctness
framing and grounded in standard GC/OT correctness and binding
assumptions~\cite{BitPriv2025,Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.
In this implementation, settlement-time checks establish anchor binding and
tuple validity, while deterministic off-chain evaluation and dispute evidence
provide computational integrity. This deployable-verification model is
deliberate and aligned with Assumption~A5. Any \texttt{outputBytes} that do not
match committed $\mathrm{hOut}_m$ are rejected by construction.
Implementation excerpts for this requirement are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:a2} and Appendix~\ref{app:d1}.

\textbf{R3. Publicly verifiable accountability.}
When a party deviates in a way covered by dispute predicates, a challenger can
provide evidence that is verifiable by the contract (directly or through
selective verification hooks), leading to deterministic blame assignment in the
BitPriv-style accountability model~\cite{BitPriv2025,Zhu2019PV2PC}.
Implementation excerpts for dispute and slashing are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:a3}, Appendix~\ref{app:b3}, and
Appendix~\ref{app:c1}--\ref{app:c2}.

\textbf{R4. Liveness with penalties.}
A single uncooperative party should not be able to block progress indefinitely.
If progress is blocked, timeout paths must release funds and/or apply penalties
according to predefined rules.

\textbf{R5. Application-level settlement integrity.}
Final payout execution must be unambiguous and consistent with the agreed settlement function $g(z)$.

\textbf{R6. Minimal information leakage in enforcement.}
Dispute and settlement data published on-chain should be limited to what is
necessary for enforceability.

\subsection{Proof Sketches for R1--R3}
Under Assumptions A1--A5, this thesis does not reproduce a full formal proof.
Instead, it adopts standard GC/OT security properties together with a
BitPriv-style publicly verifiable covert-security structure as the proof basis
for the implemented enforcement
model~\cite{BitPriv2025,Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008,Zhu2019PV2PC}.
Failures that would require violating Assumptions A1--A5 are outside the claim
scope.

\textbf{R1 (Input privacy).}
Input privacy inherits from the GC/OT construction.
The garbler observes commitments and OT-level transcript information, but does
not learn evaluator input bits; the evaluator receives only input labels
selected by its private OT choices and cannot recover garbler input beyond
output-implied leakage.
In the implemented workflow, any additional visibility is limited to mandatory
on-chain disclosures needed for enforcement.
Therefore, under standard GC privacy and OT security assumptions (A1),
evaluator and garbler inputs remain hidden except for prescribed output leakage
and explicit enforcement disclosures.

\textbf{R2 (Output correctness and binding).}
GC correctness implies that honestly generated and evaluated circuits return
$f(x,y)$.
In the implementation, settlement is anchor-bound to the selected instance
$m$: the contract accepts only \texttt{outputBytes} consistent with committed
$\mathrm{hOut}_m$ and output-domain validity checks.
If a party submits \texttt{outputBytes} inconsistent with the committed anchor,
settlement is rejected; if opened-instance data are inconsistent, deterministic
seed-based reconstruction (A2) and dispute predicates expose the inconsistency.
Hence, under GC correctness, commitment binding, hash collision resistance, and
correct EVM execution (A1, A5), accepted settlement corresponds to an
anchor-bound protocol output.

\textbf{R3 (Publicly verifiable accountability).}
Accountability follows from deterministic reconstruction plus publicly
checkable predicates for covered deviations.
For opened instances, challengers provide seed-derived artifacts, gate-leaf
evidence, and inclusion/transcript-root proofs; the contract recomputes the
relevant predicate and assigns blame deterministically.
If a dishonest deviation covered by the implemented predicates were not
attributable, then either commitment/hash binding fails, deterministic
reconstruction fails, or contract execution is incorrect---all excluded by
Assumptions A1--A5.
This yields selective public accountability for deviations covered by the
implemented dispute hooks.
Taken together, these sketches justify that the implemented protocol inherits
standard 2PC security properties while adapting enforcement to Ethereum
constraints.

\section{Economic-Security Requirements}
Security claim (2PC core): deviations that can be evidenced through verifiable
transcript checks should be economically dominated by honest execution.
Engineering choice (instantiation): this dominance is enforced through
deposits, slashing, and deadline rules in the contract layer. Beyond
cryptographic correctness, the thesis therefore requires \emph{economic
deterrence}: honest behavior should dominate profitable deviation in
expectation~\cite{BitPriv2025,Zhu2019PV2PC}.
For a deviation strategy $\delta$, define
\[
U_{\delta}=G_{\delta}-p_{\mathrm{det}}\cdot L_{\delta}-C_{\delta},
\]
where $G_{\delta}$ is the potential gain from deviation,
$p_{\mathrm{det}}$ is the probability of successful detection/challenge,
$L_{\delta}$ is the financial loss under penalties/slashing if detected, and
$C_{\delta}$ is the operational cost of executing the deviation.
For garbler-side cheating analysis, $p_{\mathrm{det},G}$ denotes detection
probability induced by dispute predicates and enforcement checks (for example,
GC-leaf/OT-root disputes and timeout-triggered accountability).

The requirement is to parameterize deposits, slashing splits, and deadlines so
that, for relevant deviation classes,
\[
U_{\delta}\leq U_{\mathrm{honest}},
\]
and preferably $U_{\delta}<0$ for high-impact deviations.

For deterrence-oriented calibration, two practical inequalities are useful:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Garbler-side cheating deterrence:}
\[
p_{\mathrm{det},G}\cdot D_G + C_G > G_G,
\]
equivalently
\[
D_G > \frac{G_G-C_G}{p_{\mathrm{det},G}} \quad (p_{\mathrm{det},G}>0),
\]
where $D_G$ is slashable garbler collateral, $G_G$ is expected gain from a
covered cheating strategy (for example, manipulating one gate/root/output), and
$C_G$ is attacker-side operational cost.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-side false-dispute deterrence:}
\[
P_{\mathrm{false},j}+C_{\mathrm{disp},j}>B_{\mathrm{grief},j},
\]
where $P_{\mathrm{false},j}$ is the false-dispute penalty for evaluator $E_j$,
$C_{\mathrm{disp},j}$ is evaluator dispute transaction cost, and
$B_{\mathrm{grief},j}$ is expected benefit from griefing delay.
\end{enumerate}

These inequalities define the calibration method: deposit and penalty
parameters are chosen to satisfy the constraints under measured implementation
conditions.
For cut-and-choose strategies, $p_{\mathrm{det},G}$ is directly tied to opening
coverage: with one evaluation instance and the remaining instances opened, the
detection probability for inconsistencies on opened instances grows with $N$.
In the implemented PoC, $N=10$ is used as a conservative baseline that raises
opening coverage and supports practical deterrence in the evaluated scenarios.

Practically, this yields four design requirements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{False-dispute deterrence:} unsuccessful challengers must incur loss.
\item \textbf{Invalid-opening deterrence:} inconsistent openings must be slashable with sufficient penalty.
\item \textbf{Abort deterrence:} withholding near deadlines should be financially dominated by honest completion.
\item \textbf{Cost-limited griefing:} the cost to delay others should be non-negligible for the attacker and limited for honest parties.
\end{enumerate}

Quantitative calibration depends on implementation-level observables (gas
profiles, artifact sizes, and latency/availability conditions). In this chapter,
we fix the calibration method and admissible constraints; numeric values used in
the experimental study are calibration baselines rather than normative protocol
constants. For the experimental baseline, we use a concrete parameter set (for example,
$N=10$, $n=3$, collateral $=1.2$ ETH, and explicit dispute deadlines encoded in
the contract) to demonstrate feasibility under deterministic conditions. These
values are chosen conservatively to cover worst-case transaction and data
availability behavior within the dispute window.

\section{Scope Boundaries for This Thesis}
This chapter formalizes the baseline target used throughout the remainder of
the thesis:
\begin{enumerate}
\item two-role economically secure 2PC (one garbler and one evaluator) as the primary cryptographic model; multi-buyer workflows are realized as repeated pairwise runs,
\item application semantics are intentionally secondary to the protocol core; auction logic is used as a concrete mapping of output-to-settlement rules,
\item Ethereum L1 as the enforcement layer,
\item and constrained on-chain verification as the deployable enforcement model.
\end{enumerate}

The protocol is specified at the commitment/dispute/state-machine layer, while
ENS-auction rules are treated as an application-layer instantiation. Claims in
this manuscript are scoped to the implemented two-role 2PC enforcement model
and its multi-participant composition pattern. The implemented $n$-buyer
extension is treated as a valid composition of pairwise 2PC executions; native
$n$-party cryptographic constructions, production-scale market design, and full
formal game-theoretic equilibrium proofs are reserved for follow-on work.

The cryptographic core remains 2PC: each execution is between one garbler and
one evaluator. The $n$-buyer auction is an application-layer composition in
which one on-chain state machine coordinates multiple pairwise
garbler$\leftrightarrow E_j$ interactions and aggregates them into one auction
outcome. In this thesis, ``$n$-buyer'' describes the workflow and application
participant set (buyers as evaluators), not a change in the underlying 2PC
security model.

\chapter{Protocol Design}
\section{Design Objectives and Strategy}
The protocol is designed around three constraints that must hold at the same time:
\begin{enumerate}
\item private inputs are processed off-chain via garbled-circuit execution,
\item outcome enforcement remains publicly verifiable on Ethereum,
\item and deviations are deterred through deposits, slashing, and deadline-triggered fallback paths.
\end{enumerate}

This chapter specifies the protocol as designed and implemented in this thesis.
It presents an Ethereum-oriented state machine with
implementation-constrained checks, keeps expensive cryptographic work
off-chain, and reserves on-chain logic for commitment binding, selective
dispute predicates, and deterministic payout/assignment transitions.

\section{Protocol Artifacts and State}
For each cut-and-choose instance $i\in\{0,\dots,N-1\}$, the garbler commits to
the tuple:
\[
(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i).
\]
Each field has a specific role:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{comSeed}_i$}: binding hash of the garbling seed for instance $i$.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{rootGC}_i$}: terminal incremental-hash state over garbled-circuit artifacts used by the GC dispute predicate.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i$}: EIP-4844 blob hash commitment for evaluation payload publication in the evaluation instance~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{hOut}_i$}: output anchor
\[
\mathrm{hOut}_i = H(\texttt{"OUT"},\mathrm{circuitId},i,\mathrm{outputBytes}).
\]
\end{enumerate}

For each evaluator $E_j$ and instance $i$, the garbler additionally commits evaluator-scoped OT transcript roots:
\[
\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}.
\]
These roots become challengeable on opened instances through deterministic
recomputation from revealed seed material (implementation excerpts:
Appendix~\ref{app:c1}--\ref{app:c2}).

The verifier-side seed is produced from evaluator commit-reveal inputs. Let
$s_j$ be the revealed seed of evaluator $E_j$ (or $0$ when defaulted by liveness
logic). The protocol derives
\[
\mathrm{verifierSeed}=\bigoplus_j s_j,
\]
and selects the evaluation instance deterministically as
\[
m = H(\texttt{"M"},\mathrm{verifierSeed},\mathrm{circuitId},\mathrm{contractAddress}) \bmod N.
\]
All non-selected instances form the opened set $S_{\mathrm{open}}$ used for
cut-and-choose verification.

\section{Phase-by-Phase Protocol Flow}
\subsection{Pre-Phase: Participant Set}
Before deposits start, the garbler registers additional evaluators (buyers in
the ENS-auction instantiation). This freezes the evaluator set for the run,
which in turn binds each participant to a public receiver address used in final
assignment.

\subsection{Phase 1: Deposits}
The garbler and all registered evaluators lock collateral. If deposits are not
complete by the deadline, refund paths allow participants to recover funds.
Successful completion advances the protocol to verifier-seed handling.

\subsection{Phase 2: Evaluator Seed Commit-Reveal and Evaluation Index}
Each evaluator first commits a hash of $(\mathrm{seed},\mathrm{salt})$, then
reveals $(\mathrm{seed},\mathrm{salt})$ in the reveal window. Missing commits or
reveals trigger default-to-zero seed with slashing to the garbler under current
contract rules. Once all evaluators are resolved (ready or defaulted), the
contract finalizes $\mathrm{verifierSeed}$ and computes $m$ deterministically.

This phase prevents one-party late selection of the evaluation index and, for
the same reason, makes cut-and-choose selection reproducible from on-chain
state.

\subsection{Phase 3: Garbler Commitments}
Phase 3 contains two contract-enforced steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Core commitments:} the garbler submits $(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i)$ for all $i$.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT commitments:} for every evaluator $E_j$, the garbler submits $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ for all $i$.
\end{enumerate}
If the garbler misses these deadlines, evaluators can trigger an abort path that
reimburses evaluator collateral and transfers garbler collateral according to
the contract rules.

\subsection{Phase 4: Evaluator Liveness and Input Readiness}
Each evaluator must confirm readiness before deadline. Evaluators that remain
unresolved are defaulted to semantic input $0$ and slashed. This guarantees
progress to the opening phase even when some participants do not cooperate.

\subsection{Phase 5: Cut-and-Choose Openings}
The garbler reveals $N-1$ seeds for indices in $S_{\mathrm{open}}$ and proves
consistency with $\mathrm{comSeed}_i$. Failure to reveal consistently or on time
leads to evaluator-triggerable penalty paths.

After valid openings, only ready evaluators remain active for dispute closure.
This is important in the $n$-buyer setting because defaulted participants do not
block dispute completion.

\subsection{Phase 6: Dispute Window}
During the dispute window, ready evaluators may open one of two explicit
challenge tracks:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GC leaf dispute:} challenge one gate leaf on an opened instance using
\begin{enumerate}
\item revealed instance seed,
\item claimed gate leaf bytes,
\item incremental-hash proof to $\mathrm{rootGC}_i$,
\item layout proof to the circuit layout commitment.
\end{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT root dispute:} for evaluator $E_j$ and opened instance $i$, recompute expected $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ from revealed seeds and domain-separated identifiers; mismatch slashes garbler, false challenge slashes evaluator.
\end{enumerate}
Implementation excerpts for both dispute tracks are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:a3}, Appendix~\ref{app:b2}, Appendix~\ref{app:b3}, and
Appendix~\ref{app:c1}--\ref{app:c2}.

The protocol advances from dispute to label reveal when all active evaluators
close their dispute state or when the dispute deadline expires.

\subsection{Phase 7: Label Reveal and Evaluation Payload Binding}
The garbler reveals garbler labels for the evaluation instance $m$. In the same
transition, the contract checks that \texttt{blobhash(0)} equals committed
$\mathrm{blobHashGC}_m$. This binds settlement to one concrete EIP-4844 payload
reference in that transaction (specific blob index and versioned hash).
Payload semantics are verified through off-chain reconstruction and dispute
checks, while on-chain logic enforces hash binding and phase integrity.
Because blob data is ephemeral, this implementation assumes payload
retrievability at least throughout the dispute horizon
(Assumption~A4)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
The concrete on-chain binding check is shown in Appendix~\ref{app:a4}.

\subsection{Phase 8: Settlement and Assignment}
Any participant can submit \texttt{outputBytes}. The contract accepts only if:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $H(\texttt{"OUT"},\mathrm{circuitId},m,\mathrm{outputBytes}) = \mathrm{hOut}_m$,
\item decoded fields are well-formed and within bounds (winner id, winning bid, offered name hash).
\end{enumerate}

In the current proof-of-concept encoding:
\[
\mathrm{outputBytes}=\mathrm{encodePacked}(\mathrm{winnerId},\mathrm{winningBid},\mathrm{chosenNamehash}).
\]
After acceptance, the contract applies first-price transfer logic (winner pays
own bid from collateral vault), records winner metadata, and transitions to
final ENS assignment and payout settlement.
Accepted settlement in this implementation certifies anchor-consistent and
well-formed output bytes under the protocol's selective-verification model.
Implementation excerpts are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:a2}.

\section{n-Evaluator Generalization (Auction Instantiation: n Buyers)}
Relative to a single-evaluator baseline, the protocol generalizes by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item evaluator-scoped OT commitments $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ instead of one global OT root,
\item per-evaluator liveness/default handling in Phase 4,
\item per-evaluator dispute closure tracking in Phase 6,
\item and deterministic $m$ derived from aggregated evaluator seed material.
\end{enumerate}
This keeps the protocol backbone unchanged while allowing one run to include
multiple competing evaluators (buyers in the auction application) under a
common commitment/dispute framework.

\section{Protocol vs Application Layer}
The protocol core is intentionally application-agnostic: commitments, openings,
disputes, deadlines, and settlement anchors are independent of specific
business rules. The ENS auction logic is the current application mapping:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the computed output determines winner identity and winning bid,
\item final assignment binds the chosen ENS name hash to the winner's registered receiver,
\item and first-price payment is executed from vault balances.
\end{enumerate}

The implementation currently models a fixed shortlist of offered ENS name hashes
and validates that the chosen output name belongs to that offered set. This is
an engineering simplification of the broader application vision and does not
change the protocol-level enforcement structure.

\section{Specification Conformance}
For reproducibility, the protocol notation and ordering in this chapter follow
the implemented state machine.
Output validity is specified via the structured anchor
$\mathrm{hOut}$ over packed output bytes, and the phase order places
verifier-seed finalization before the garbler's core commitment submission.
These choices keep the specification directly consistent with the contract-level
transition logic used in the prototype.

\section{On-Chain Architecture}
\subsection{State Machine and Core Objects}
At contract level, the core 2PC enforcement contract models the protocol as an explicit finite state machine with hard deadlines.
The stages include deposits, evaluator seed commit/reveal, core commitments,
evaluator-scoped OT commitments, evaluator liveness finalization, opening,
dispute, label reveal, settlement, assignment, and closure.

Protocol-critical commitment objects are stored per cut-and-choose instance:
\[
(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i),
\]
and per evaluator-instance OT commitments:
\[
\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}.
\]
This state layout directly supports dispute traceability and deterministic verification for opened instances.

\subsection{Phase Handlers}
The contract exposes dedicated handlers for each transition class:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Seed handling and evaluation index derivation:} evaluator seed commit and reveal finalization, plus deterministic $m$ selection.
\item \textbf{Commitment publication:} the garbler submits core commitments, then OT roots per evaluator.
\item \textbf{Liveness progression:} unresolved evaluators are defaulted and slashed after deadline, preserving protocol progress.
\item \textbf{Opening and dispute entry:} the garbler reveals $N-1$ openings; active evaluators enter dispute window.
\item \textbf{Settlement and assignment:} output anchor validation, vault updates, winner recording, and final assignment.
\end{enumerate}

This implementation structure keeps transition logic auditable on-chain and cross-phase behavior unambiguous.

\subsection{Dispute and Accountability Mechanism}
Two explicit dispute predicates are implemented on-chain:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GC leaf dispute:} verifies one challenged gate leaf on an opened instance via seed-consistent recomputation, incremental-hash proof, and layout proof.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT root dispute:} recomputes expected OT root for one evaluator and one opened instance; mismatch slashes garbler, false challenge slashes evaluator.
\end{enumerate}

The dispute window closes either when all active evaluators close their dispute
state or when deadline-based finalization is triggered.
The design provides constrained on-chain verification while preserving economically enforceable blame assignment.

\subsection{Settlement and Assignment Path}
Settlement accepts an output only if the computed anchor equals committed $\mathrm{hOut}_m$ for the evaluation instance.
It then decodes the accepted output into winner id, winning bid, and chosen name hash.
Next, the contract applies first-price vault transfer logic and executes assignment in a separate finalization step.
This separation keeps settlement correctness checks explicit and makes payout/assignment transitions observable on-chain.

function challengeGateLeaf(
    uint256 instanceId,
    uint256 gateIndex,
    GateDesc calldata g,
    bytes calldata leafBytes,
    bytes32[] calldata ihProof,
    bytes32[] calldata layoutProof
) public {
    require(currentStage == Stage.Dispute, "Wrong stage");
    require(block.timestamp <= deadlines.dispute, "Dispute deadline missed");
    require(isBuyer[msg.sender], "Only buyer for dispute");
    require(buyerStatus[msg.sender] == BuyerStatus.Ready, "Buyer not active in dispute");

    _assertValidLayoutProof(gateIndex, g, layoutProof);
    bytes32 seed = _requireRevealedSeedForOpenedInstance(instanceId);
    bytes32 leafHash = _gateLeafHash(gateIndex, leafBytes);
    require(_processIncrementalProof(leafHash, ihProof) == instanceCommitments[instanceId].rootGC, "Bad IH proof");

    bytes memory expected = recomputeGateLeafBytes(seed, instanceId, gateIndex, g);
    bool matchLeaf = (_gateLeafHash(gateIndex, expected) == leafHash);
    if (!matchLeaf) _slashAliceToAllBuyers();
    else _slashBuyerToAlice(msg.sender);
}

\section{Implementation Design Choices and Scope}
This thesis uses a proof-of-concept implementation to validate end-to-end
protocol enforceability.
Three choices are deliberate protocol-engineering decisions:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Shared deterministic GC/OT rules across Solidity and Rust:} the constrained canonical representation is required to guarantee verifier parity.
\item \textbf{Constrained on-chain verification:} leaf-level and root-level checks are chosen to keep verification gas-limited and deployable on Ethereum.
\item \textbf{Hash-bound evaluation payload publication:} blob-hash commitment checks enforce data binding for settlement while keeping heavy data handling off the contract path~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\end{enumerate}

The implementation therefore serves as an end-to-end feasibility validation of
the protocol model under Ethereum constraints.

\item \textbf{Off-chain deterministic tests:} off-chain-common integration tests check parity and encoding. Coverage includes cut-and-choose and OT flows, settlement-anchor derivation, eval-blob checks, and EIP-4844 hash derivation.
\item \textbf{End-to-end scenario script:} dedicated demo coverage for honest success, evaluator default, and garbler-cheat dispute flows.

Together, these three validation layers are the reproducibility baseline for the implemented protocol and its ENS auction application path.

\subsection{Execution Environment}
Evaluation runs were executed on a local deterministic Anvil chain.
Two harnesses were used: an automated scenario harness

For the measured baseline, the protocol parameters were:
\begin{enumerate}
\item number of cut-and-choose instances: $N=10$,
\item number of evaluators (buyers in the ENS application): $n=3$,
\item circuit bit width: $57$ (chosen so the bid domain for this baseline stays below the 1.2 ETH collateral cap),
\item collateral per participant: $1.2$ ETH,
\item auction rule: first-price settlement with deterministic winner decoding.
\end{enumerate}

The role split between parameters is intentional:
\(N\) controls cut-and-choose verification coverage, while \(n\) sets the size
of the auction instantiation.

Local deterministic execution gives reproducible behavior and cost traces.
Production fee calibration still depends on deployment-specific gas-price and
blob-fee assumptions.

\section{Threats to Validity}
The evidence is strong for implementation-level protocol behavior, but external
validity remains bounded:
\begin{enumerate}
\item local execution does not model live mempool competition or adversarial
transaction ordering,
\item gas values here are execution-cost measurements, not final fee
predictions under volatile base-fee/blob-fee conditions,
\item the \(n,N\) sweep is moderate and trend-oriented; broad throughput limits
are out of scope,
\item off-chain timing values depend on local hardware and implementation
maturity,
\item debugging output in the experimental execution harness is not part of the
privacy-preserving protocol interface.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}
\section{Discussion of the Main Findings}
We set out to design and validate an economically secure 2PC protocol for
Ethereum, with sealed-bid auctions as the concrete application context. The
implementation and evaluation show that this objective is feasible when
enforcement is encoded as a strict on-chain state machine while heavy private
computation remains off-chain.

Three implemented findings are central.
First, \textbf{output binding and settlement integrity} are enforceable through
anchor checks (\texttt{hOut}) and deterministic output decoding constraints.
Second, \textbf{accountability under deviation} is achieved through scoped
dispute predicates (GC-leaf and evaluator-scoped OT-root tracks) with
deterministic slashing outcomes.
Third, \textbf{liveness under strategic behavior} is preserved through
deadline-driven default and penalty transitions.

Taken together, these findings indicate that privacy-preserving computation can
be embedded into practical Ethereum settlement workflows through commitments,
deadlines, and selective challenge predicates, without requiring universal
on-chain re-execution.

The implementation process also surfaced three design lessons. Deployability
constraints are first-order protocol parameters, cost-aware verification design
is necessary for L1 feasibility, and protocol simplicity materially improves
end-to-end implementability and auditability.

These conclusions remain consistent with publicly verifiable financially secure
2PC directions in prior work, while grounding them in a concrete,
deployment-oriented enforcement model for Ethereum~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC,BitPriv2025}.

\section{Protocol-Core vs Application Layer}
A key architectural choice is the separation between a reusable protocol core
and application-specific market logic.
Commitment lifecycle, deadlines, dispute predicates, and settlement binding are
protocol-core mechanisms; the ENS auction is one application-layer
instantiation over that backbone, realized here by first-price settlement and
deterministic assignment of the selected namehash.

This separation improves architectural transferability while limiting
application-specific assumptions in the protocol-core security and enforcement
model.
The same core can be instantiated for sealed tenders, private matching,
confidential allocation, or selective procurement, provided outcomes can be
encoded into anchor-bound settlement bytes with deterministic execution rules.
It also enables auction-mechanism upgrades at the application layer without
redesigning the enforcement core.

\section{Limitations}
The contribution is established within explicit technical boundaries:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Incentive model scope:} the protocol enforces deposits, deadlines,
and challenge costs, and demonstrates deterministic slash/default outcomes in
representative adversarial flows; it does not provide a full formal equilibrium
proof (e.g., Nash or subgame-perfect) over all strategic actor behaviors.
\item \textbf{Deployment economics scope:} gas values are measured under
deterministic local execution and support reproducible execution-cost
comparison; they are not direct production fee predictions under dynamic
base-fee, priority-fee, and blob-fee conditions.
\item \textbf{Verification scope:} the enforcement model is based on
bounded/selective dispute predicates and settlement-time binding checks, rather
than universal on-chain re-execution of the complete private computation.
\item \textbf{Multi-party scope:} multi-buyer auction behavior is implemented as
application-layer composition of pairwise 2PC executions, not as a native
\(n\)-party MPC protocol.
\item \textbf{Benchmark scope:} the evaluation validates scenario correctness and
moderate scaling trends in \(n\) and \(N\), but does not claim production
throughput ceilings or adversarial network-stress limits.
\item \textbf{Application-mechanism scope:} the ENS instantiation demonstrates
architectural transferability, but does not establish welfare-optimality or
incentive properties across broader private auction mechanisms.
\end{enumerate}

These boundaries clarify what is demonstrated and measured in this thesis, and
they define a precise extension surface for follow-on work.

\section{Future Work}
Near-term engineering work concerns deployment calibration and broader empirical
validation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Production fee calibration and deployment-level benchmarking:} map measured execution gas to deployment fee assumptions (base fee, priority fee, blob fee) and evaluate cost robustness across realistic network conditions~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\item \textbf{Robustness and stress validation:} extend scenario campaigns to
larger evaluator sets and richer adversarial combinations, including
adversarial timing and execution ordering conditions.
\item \textbf{Tooling and automation:} strengthen reproducibility through tighter
measurement pipelines, scenario orchestration, and regression automation for
cost, dispute, and settlement invariants.
\item \textbf{Broader empirical scaling:} expand \(n,N\)-sweep coverage while
preserving deterministic harness semantics and comparable reporting criteria.
\end{enumerate}

Longer-term research directions concern formal strategic guarantees and protocol
generalization:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Formal strategic analysis:} derive explicit equilibrium-level
conditions for collateral, deadlines, and challenge pricing under richer
attacker models.
\item \textbf{Native multi-party variants:} evaluate protocol families that
directly realize \(n\)-party private computation while preserving public
enforceability semantics.
\item \textbf{Richer private market mechanisms:} generalize beyond the current
first-price ENS mapping toward alternative allocation and pricing rules.
\item \textbf{Stronger privacy/composability models:} refine compositional
security arguments for combined off-chain computation, selective dispute
exposure, and repeated-market operation.
\item \textbf{Alternative settlement layers:} compare trust/performance tradeoffs
of additional blockchain settlement environments and cross-layer architectures
while preserving the enforcement-first contribution of this work~\cite{Haloani2024COTIv2}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
The central result is that privacy-preserving market execution on Ethereum can
be achieved through economically enforceable off-chain computation, without
requiring full on-chain private computation.

Our implemented Solidity/Rust prototype demonstrates that commitments,
deadlines, selective disputes, and deterministic settlement can be composed
into a coherent L1 enforcement workflow under a concrete auction
instantiation.
This establishes a deployment-oriented design pattern for private market
mechanisms on public blockchains.

Overall, we provide a credible L1-native path toward enforceable private market
execution through disciplined protocol engineering and economic alignment.
