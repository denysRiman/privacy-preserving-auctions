\begin{abstract}
This thesis studies how economically secure two-party computation (2PC) can be
realized on Ethereum for private bidding protocols. The core challenge is a
structural tension of public blockchains: transparency enables auditability but
also exposes transaction data and ordering opportunities, which can undermine
fairness and correctness in sealed-bid settings.

The research question is how to design a protocol that simultaneously provides
(i) bid privacy before valid settlement, (ii) publicly enforceable execution,
(iii) robustness against delay and abort behavior, and (iv) practical
deployability under Ethereum constraints. The proposed approach combines
off-chain garbled-circuit/OT execution with an on-chain enforcement state
machine. The contract governs phase transitions, commitments, deadlines,
dispute entry points, and deterministic settlement rules.

The work delivers a Solidity+Rust proof of concept and evaluates it through
end-to-end scenarios (honest run, evaluator-defaulted liveness path, and
garbler-cheat dispute path). Results show that structured output-anchor binding
(\texttt{hOut}), bounded publicly verifiable dispute predicates, and
deadline-based default/slashing logic can be integrated into one coherent
protocol flow. This demonstrates practical feasibility of economically secure
2PC on Ethereum at the protocol-core layer. An ENS auction mapping is
implemented as an application layer over the same enforcement backbone.

The thesis also states clear boundaries: full production-grade gas calibration
and deeper economic parameter optimization remain future work.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
\section{Context and Motivation}
Public blockchains provide strong auditability, but this property comes with a direct tradeoff: transaction data and transaction ordering are visible before final settlement. On Ethereum, this transparency enables adversarial strategies such as reordering, insertion, and censorship, often discussed under maximal extractable value (MEV)~\cite{Wood2025Ethereum,Daian2019FlashBoys}. For protocols that involve private inputs, this visibility is not a side issue; it directly affects fairness and correctness of outcomes.

This thesis studies that tension for a concrete and practically relevant setting: sealed-bid auctions. In this setting, bids should remain private during execution, while the final winner and settlement must be publicly enforceable. If private information leaks too early, strategic behavior can distort the outcome and reduce trust in the mechanism. If enforcement is weak, parties can deviate or abort at points where deviation is economically beneficial.

Recent work by Alexopoulos et al. (BitPriv) demonstrates that privacy-preserving and economically enforceable computation can be achieved in a Bitcoin-native environment through off-chain two-party computation (2PC), collateral-backed incentives, and on-chain dispute resolution~\cite{BitPriv2025}. This thesis addresses the Ethereum setting directly and develops a protocol and prototype tailored to Ethereum execution constraints, with explicit state-machine enforcement, bounded disputes, and deterministic settlement rules.

\section{Problem Statement}
The central problem is to design a protocol for sealed-bid auctions on Ethereum that satisfies four properties at the same time:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Input privacy}: bids are not revealed before the protocol reaches a valid settlement point.
\item \textbf{Public enforceability}: the on-chain contract can enforce the agreed output and financial transfers.
\item \textbf{Robustness to strategic behavior}: the protocol remains operational when parties stall, abort, or provide inconsistent artifacts.
\item \textbf{Practical deployability}: on-chain overhead remains manageable and implementation complexity stays within realistic engineering bounds.
\end{enumerate}

In public-blockchain deployments these goals interact in non-trivial ways. Stronger cryptographic guarantees may increase operational cost, while cheaper constructions can weaken deterrence against rational deviations. The protocol therefore has to be reasoned about not only cryptographically, but also economically: the expected payoff of honest behavior should dominate profitable deviation under explicit assumptions~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC}.

This thesis follows an \emph{optimistic, dispute-driven enforcement} model.
On-chain logic enforces deadlines, deposits, slashing, settlement-anchor checks,
and bounded dispute predicates. Off-chain logic performs the full GC/OT
evaluation and derives \texttt{outputBytes}. Therefore, the contract proves
enforceability and binding, while computational correctness is established via
off-chain execution plus dispute evidence rather than full on-chain
re-execution.

\section{Research Objectives}
Based on the approved thesis proposal, the objective of this work is to design and prototype an economically secure 2PC protocol for Ethereum-based sealed-bid auctions, with first-price auctions as the baseline use case.

Concretely, the thesis targets:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a protocol architecture that combines off-chain 2PC execution with on-chain commitments, deadlines, and disputes;
\item a threat and assumptions model suitable for adversarial transaction environments;
\item a Solidity + Rust proof-of-concept demonstrating end-to-end execution;
\item an evaluation of feasibility, including cost/performance characteristics and dispute-path practicality;
\item an economic-security analysis that explains how deposits, collateral, and penalties deter rational cheating.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Contributions of This Thesis}
The expected contributions of this thesis are the following:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Ethereum-oriented protocol design:} a protocol that preserves bid privacy while keeping settlement publicly enforceable in a hostile execution environment.
\item \textbf{Economic-security framing:} a practical incentive model for rational participants, including explicit assumptions and deterrence conditions.
\item \textbf{Prototype implementation:} an integrated proof-of-concept with on-chain contract logic and off-chain 2PC components.
\item \textbf{Empirical evidence:} implementation-driven measurements and scenario-based analysis of optimistic and dispute executions.
\item \textbf{Ethereum-specific protocol engineering:} a concrete protocol realization for Ethereum with explicit phase transitions, enforceable dispute predicates, and settlement logic.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Background and Related Work}
\section{Secure Two-Party Computation Basics}
Secure two-party computation (2PC) studies how two mutually distrustful parties can jointly compute a function over private inputs while revealing only the prescribed output. In this thesis, the technical baseline follows the garbled-circuit line of work, where one party garbles a circuit and the other evaluates it using input labels obtained via oblivious transfer (OT). Foundational properties, formal treatment, and malicious-security efficiency tradeoffs are documented in the classical and modern literature~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.

For blockchain applications, 2PC is attractive because most heavy cryptographic computation can remain off-chain, while on-chain logic is used primarily for commitments, deadlines, and settlement enforcement. This architecture matches the need to reduce gas cost while still preserving privacy and accountability.

\section{From Semi-Honest to Malicious Security}
In a semi-honest model, parties follow the protocol but try to learn extra information from transcripts. This assumption is often insufficient for financial applications, where strategic deviation is economically motivated. Upgrading to malicious security introduces additional mechanisms, for example cut-and-choose checks for garbling correctness, commitment consistency checks, and explicit abort handling~\cite{LindellPinkas2008}.

The key engineering tension is that stronger malicious-security guarantees usually increase communication and verification overhead. In public-blockchain settings, this overhead directly translates into on-chain cost and operational complexity. Therefore, protocol design must balance cryptographic rigor with deployability constraints.

\section{Public Verifiability and Economic Enforcement}
Publicly verifiable 2PC aims to make protocol violations externally checkable, so that a third party or smart contract can arbitrate disputes without re-executing the full protocol off-chain. Zhu et al. present an influential direction where dispute resolution is integrated with blockchain-based enforcement for financially secure computation settings~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC}.

BitPriv extends this line by combining off-chain 2PC with collateral-backed on-chain punishment and publicly verifiable dispute flow in a Bitcoin-compatible framework~\cite{BitPriv2025}. In this thesis, the \emph{economic-security} viewpoint is formalized for the Ethereum setting: participants are assumed rational and may deviate if profitable, so protocol parameters (deposits, slashing rules, deadlines) must make honest execution the dominant economic strategy.

\section{Adversarial Execution on Public Blockchains}
Ethereum's execution environment is transparent by design. Mempool visibility and transaction ordering freedom create room for adversarial behavior, commonly framed as MEV-related strategies~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}. This environment is particularly problematic for auction-like protocols, where partial information leaks can influence bidding behavior and settlement outcomes.

At the same time, Ethereum's public verifiability and programmable contracts provide the enforcement substrate needed for commitment checks, dispute escalation, and deterministic settlement rules. The challenge is therefore not only to hide sensitive inputs, but to do so while preserving enforceable and cost-bounded execution under adversarial ordering~\cite{Wood2025Ethereum}.

\section{Privacy-Preserving Auctions on Blockchains}
Private auction design on public ledgers has been explored from multiple angles. FAST targets fair auctions using secret transaction techniques~\cite{David2021FAST}. In parallel, systems research explores general private computation layers for blockchain applications. gcVM proposes publicly auditable MPC-compatible execution ideas for EVM-like environments~\cite{Yanai2026gcVM}, while COTI V2 explores a confidentiality-oriented layer-2 approach with additional system assumptions~\cite{Haloani2024COTIv2}.

These approaches collectively show that confidentiality and public enforceability can be combined, but with different trust, complexity, and cost profiles. This thesis focuses on an L1-oriented Ethereum path with explicit economic incentives and dispute handling, aiming to minimize additional trust assumptions beyond the base chain.

\section{Deployment Considerations for Ethereum}
A practical protocol for Ethereum must account for execution costs and data availability paths. In particular, if large off-chain artifacts (for example, evaluation payloads derived from garbled-circuit flow) are referenced by on-chain commitments, the protocol must specify how these artifacts are made available and bound to settlement logic. EIP-4844 introduces blob transactions as a relevant mechanism for high-volume data publication with different cost characteristics than calldata~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.

Accordingly, protocol design choices in this thesis are evaluated not only for security semantics, but also for concrete deployment feasibility under Ethereum's transaction and fee model.

\section{Positioning of This Thesis}
Relative to prior work, this thesis is positioned at the intersection of:
\begin{enumerate}
\item garbled-circuit-based 2PC foundations~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008},
\item publicly verifiable and financially motivated enforcement models~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC,BitPriv2025},
\item adversarial blockchain execution analysis with MEV-aware reasoning~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys,Wood2025Ethereum},
\item and private auction protocol design for practical deployment~\cite{David2021FAST,Yanai2026gcVM,Haloani2024COTIv2,Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\end{enumerate}

The main difference from existing systems is the target setting: economically secure 2PC for sealed-bid auctions on Ethereum, with an explicit implementation and evaluation path that aligns protocol assumptions with engineering reality.

\chapter{Model and Requirements}
\section{System Model and Roles}
This chapter uses an explicit layer vocabulary to avoid mixing protocol and
application semantics:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Protocol (2PC core):} pairwise GC/OT execution between one
garbler $G$ (private input $x$) and one evaluator $E$ (private input $y$), with
transcript commitments and bounded dispute predicates~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.
\item \textbf{Contract enforcement layer:} on-chain state machine for deadlines,
deposits, slashing, and bounded verification checks. The contract is an
enforcement mechanism, not a cryptographic party.
\item \textbf{Application instantiation:} auction-specific aggregation logic
(``many evaluators'' in protocol terms; ``buyers'' in the ENS application),
winner decoding, settlement formatting, and ENS assignment that consume already
bound outputs.
\end{enumerate}

Terminology used in this chapter is fixed as follows:
``2PC protocol'' means one GC/OT execution between a garbler and one evaluator,
while ``$n$-buyer auction'' means application-layer composition of multiple such
runs (one per evaluator/buyer) plus deterministic aggregation.

At protocol-core level, the target computation is deterministic:
\[
z=f(x,y).
\]
In the auction instantiation, multiple pairwise runs are orchestrated and their
outputs are aggregated by application logic. The auction logic is not part of
the 2PC protocol; it is an application that consumes the protocol's bound
output.

Protocol-level privacy is defined per pairwise run: the garbler should not learn
the evaluator's private input beyond what is implied by the agreed output. In
the instantiated multi-buyer workflow, buyers should not learn other buyers'
bids beyond output-implied information. These claims assume correct off-chain
channel and artifact handling; operational leak vectors such as debug logs,
local storage exposure, or external telemetry are outside the cryptographic
guarantee boundary.

\section{Contract-Orchestration State Model}
The following phases describe contract orchestration for the chosen
instantiation. They are enforcement/liveness stages, not cryptographic 2PC
primitives:
\begin{enumerate}
\item deposits,
\item evaluator-seed commit and reveal,
\item core commitments and evaluator-scoped OT-root commitments,
\item evaluator liveness/input finalization,
\item cut-and-choose opening,
\item dispute window,
\item label reveal and settlement submission,
\item assignment/final payout or timeout resolution.
\end{enumerate}

For each circuit instance $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$, the model includes commitment
objects such as seed commitments (`comSeed`), garbled-artifact roots
(`rootGC`), evaluation blob hashes (`blobHashGC`), evaluator-scoped OT roots
(`buyerRootOTCommitment`), and output anchors (`hOut`). The selected evaluation
instance is derived from finalized verifier-seed material, and output validity
is modeled as hash binding against the committed anchor for that instance.

The design objective is to keep high-volume cryptographic artifacts off-chain in normal execution, while preserving a bounded and externally verifiable dispute path. This is aligned with practical Ethereum deployment constraints and data-publication tradeoffs (including blob-based publication options)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.

\section{Adversary and Deviation Model}
We use an adversarial model that combines:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{malicious protocol behavior} at the party level (invalid commitments, inconsistent openings, selective abort),
\item \textbf{rational economic behavior} (deviation is attempted when expected utility is positive),
\item and \textbf{adversarial transaction environment} on public Ethereum (observable mempool, reorderability, latency asymmetry)~\cite{Daian2019FlashBoys}.
\end{enumerate}

The adversary can delay, reorder, or strategically time its own transactions, but cannot break standard cryptographic assumptions. The contract and chain consensus are assumed to follow Ethereum's underlying security model. In this thesis, denial-of-service at the base-consensus layer and chain-level catastrophic failures are out of scope.

Representative deviations considered in the model include:
\begin{enumerate}
\item submitting malformed commitments,
\item opening data inconsistent with previous commitments,
\item issuing false disputes to delay settlement,
\item withholding required messages near deadlines,
\item and attempting invalid output submission.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Assumptions}
\textbf{A1. Cryptographic assumptions.}
Hash functions used for commitments and anchors are collision-resistant, and the underlying garbled-circuit/OT constructions provide their expected security properties under standard assumptions~\cite{Bellare2012FoundationsGC,LindellPinkas2008}.

\textbf{A2. Deterministic reconstruction interface.}
Given agreed seeds and domain-separation inputs, honest verifiers reconstruct the same per-instance artifacts and transcript commitments. This assumption is required so disputes are objective and reproducible.

\textbf{A3. Contract-enforceable timing.}
Deadlines are represented in contract state and interpreted under Ethereum block progression (deposit, verifier-seed, commitment, evaluator-input, open, dispute, labels, settle windows). A party that misses a required action before its deadline is treated as non-cooperative and penalized by protocol rules.

\textbf{A4. Data availability during dispute windows.}
Data needed for a valid challenge is available to challengers in time, either by direct exchange or by a publication path bound to on-chain references (for example, blob-hash anchored payloads)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
For blob-linked publication paths, this is a time-bounded availability
assumption over the dispute window, not a permanent archival guarantee.
In particular, blob references are treated as timely-retrieval hints for
challengers, not as archival channels equivalent to calldata.

\textbf{A5. Bounded on-chain verification.}
Only bounded checks are expected on-chain (for example, leaf-level consistency checks), not full cryptographic re-execution of entire circuits.

\textbf{A6. Instantiation assumption: ENS adapter correctness and authority.}
For the ENS application mapping, the adapter called by the contract is assumed
to be authorized to perform assignment for the offered names and to implement
\texttt{assign(namehash,to)} correctly (without hidden side paths). Detailed ENS
ownership/registration mechanics are treated as application-integration context,
not as a protocol-core security claim of this thesis.

\section{Security Requirements}
Under the above model, requirements are separated into:
\textbf{security claims for the 2PC core} (R1--R3) and
\textbf{engineering requirements for enforcement/instantiation} (R4--R6).

\textbf{R1. Input privacy.}
No party learns the counterparty's private input beyond what is implied by the prescribed output and mandatory on-chain disclosures.

\textbf{R2. Output correctness and binding.}
An accepted settlement must correspond to a valid protocol-consistent output. Invalid output labels or inconsistent evidence must be rejectable by the contract.
In this PoC, settlement-time on-chain checks establish anchor binding and tuple
validity, but do not provide full on-chain re-execution proof of the underlying
garbled-circuit computation.
This bounded-verification boundary is a deliberate design choice aligned with
Assumption~A5.
At the MVP level, output correctness relies on deterministic off-chain
evaluation by protocol participants (implemented via shared evaluator logic),
while on-chain settlement enforces binding: if a party submits
\texttt{outputBytes} that do not match committed $\mathrm{hOut}_m$, settlement
fails by construction.

\textbf{R3. Publicly verifiable accountability.}
When a party deviates in a way covered by dispute predicates, a challenger can provide evidence that is verifiable by the contract (directly or through bounded verification hooks), leading to deterministic blame assignment~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC}.

\textbf{R4. Liveness with penalties.}
A single uncooperative party should not be able to block progress indefinitely. If progress is blocked, timeout paths must release funds and/or apply penalties according to predefined rules.

\textbf{R5. Application-level settlement integrity.}
Final payout execution must be unambiguous and consistent with the agreed settlement function $g(z)$.

\textbf{R6. Minimal information leakage in enforcement.}
Dispute and settlement data published on-chain should be limited to what is necessary for enforceability.

\section{Economic-Security Requirements}
Security claim (2PC core): deviations that can be evidenced via verifiable
transcript checks should be economically dominated by honest execution.
Engineering choice (instantiation): enforce this through deposits, slashing,
and deadline rules in the contract layer. Beyond cryptographic correctness, this
thesis therefore requires \emph{economic deterrence}: honest behavior should
dominate profitable deviation in expectation~\cite{BitPriv2025,Zhu2019PV2PC}.
For a deviation strategy $\delta$, define
\[
U_{\delta}=G_{\delta}-p_{\mathrm{det}}\cdot L_{\delta}-C_{\delta},
\]
where $G_{\delta}$ is the potential gain from deviation, $p_{\mathrm{det}}$ is the probability of successful detection/challenge, $L_{\delta}$ is the financial loss under penalties/slashing if detected, and $C_{\delta}$ is the operational cost of executing the deviation.
For garbler-side cheating analysis, $p_{\mathrm{det},G}$ denotes detection
probability through dispute predicates and enforcement checks (for example,
GC-leaf/OT-root disputes and timeout-triggered accountability), not probability
of full on-chain GC correctness verification.

The requirement is to parameterize deposits, slashing splits, and deadlines so that, for relevant deviation classes,
\[
U_{\delta}\leq U_{\mathrm{honest}},
\]
and preferably $U_{\delta}<0$ for high-impact deviations.

For deterrence-oriented calibration, two practical inequalities are useful:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Garbler-side cheating deterrence:}
\[
p_{\mathrm{det},G}\cdot D_G + C_G > G_G,
\]
equivalently
\[
D_G > \frac{G_G-C_G}{p_{\mathrm{det},G}} \quad (p_{\mathrm{det},G}>0),
\]
where $D_G$ is slashable garbler collateral, $G_G$ is expected gain from a
covered cheating strategy (for example, manipulating one gate/root/output), and
$C_G$ is attacker-side operational cost.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-side false-dispute deterrence:}
\[
P_{\mathrm{false},j}+C_{\mathrm{disp},j}>B_{\mathrm{grief},j},
\]
where $P_{\mathrm{false},j}$ is the false-dispute penalty for evaluator $E_j$,
$C_{\mathrm{disp},j}$ is evaluator dispute transaction cost, and
$B_{\mathrm{grief},j}$ is expected benefit from griefing delay.
\end{enumerate}

These inequalities define the calibration method: deposit/penalty parameters are
chosen to satisfy these constraints under measured implementation conditions.
For cut-and-choose strategies, $p_{\mathrm{det},G}$ is directly tied to opening
coverage: with one evaluation instance and the remaining instances opened, the
detection probability for inconsistencies on opened instances grows with $N$.
In the implemented PoC, $N=10$ is used as a conservative baseline that increases
opening coverage and supports practical deterrence in the evaluated scenarios.

Practically, this yields four design requirements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{False-dispute deterrence:} unsuccessful challengers must incur loss.
\item \textbf{Invalid-opening deterrence:} inconsistent openings must be slashable with sufficient penalty.
\item \textbf{Abort deterrence:} withholding near deadlines should be financially dominated by honest completion.
\item \textbf{Bounded griefing:} the cost to delay others should be non-negligible for the attacker and bounded for honest parties.
\end{enumerate}

Quantitative calibration depends on implementation-level observables (gas
profiles, artifact sizes, and latency/availability conditions). In this chapter,
we fix the calibration method and admissible constraints; numeric values used in
the PoC are illustrative baselines rather than normative protocol constants.
For the experimental baseline, we use a concrete PoC parameter set (for example,
$N=10$, $n=3$, collateral $=1.2$ ETH, and explicit dispute deadlines encoded in
the contract) to demonstrate feasibility under deterministic conditions. These
values are chosen conservatively to cover worst-case transaction and data
availability behavior within the dispute window.

\section{Scope Boundaries for This Thesis}
This chapter formalizes the baseline target used in the remainder of the thesis:
\begin{enumerate}
\item two-role economically secure 2PC (one garbler and one evaluator) as the primary cryptographic model; multi-buyer workflows are realized as repeated pairwise runs,
\item application semantics are intentionally secondary to the protocol core; auction logic is used as a concrete mapping of output-to-settlement rules,
\item Ethereum L1 as the enforcement layer,
\item and bounded on-chain verification instead of full on-chain re-execution.
\end{enumerate}

In particular, the protocol is specified at the commitment/dispute/state-machine
layer, while ENS-auction rules are treated as an application-layer
instantiation. Claims in this manuscript are bounded to the implemented
two-role 2PC enforcement model and its multi-participant composition pattern.
The implemented $n$-buyer extension is treated as a valid composition of
pairwise 2PC executions; native $n$-party cryptographic constructions,
production-scale market design, and full formal game-theoretic equilibrium
proofs are outside the present claim scope.

The cryptographic core remains 2PC: each execution is between one garbler and
one evaluator. The $n$-buyer auction is an application-layer composition: one
on-chain state machine coordinates multiple pairwise
garbler$\leftrightarrow E_j$ interactions and aggregates them into one auction
outcome. In this thesis, ``$n$-buyer'' describes workflow and application
participant set (buyers as evaluators),
not a change in the underlying 2PC security model.

\chapter{Protocol Design}
\section{Design Objectives and Strategy}
The protocol is designed around three constraints that must hold simultaneously:
\begin{enumerate}
\item private inputs are processed off-chain via garbled-circuit execution,
\item outcome enforcement remains publicly verifiable on Ethereum,
\item and deviations are deterred through deposits, slashing, and deadline-triggered fallback paths.
\end{enumerate}

This chapter specifies the protocol designed and implemented in this thesis. It defines an Ethereum-oriented state machine with implementation-constrained checks, keeping expensive cryptographic work off-chain and reserving on-chain logic for commitment binding, bounded dispute predicates, and deterministic payout/assignment transitions.

\section{Protocol Artifacts and State}
For each cut-and-choose instance $i\in\{0,\dots,N-1\}$, the garbler commits a tuple:
\[
(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i).
\]
The fields have the following role:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{comSeed}_i$}: binding hash of the garbling seed for instance $i$.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{rootGC}_i$}: terminal incremental-hash state over garbled-circuit artifacts used by the GC dispute predicate.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i$}: EIP-4844 blob hash commitment for evaluation payload publication in the evaluation instance~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\item \textbf{$\mathrm{hOut}_i$}: output anchor
\[
\mathrm{hOut}_i = H(\texttt{"OUT"},\mathrm{circuitId},i,\mathrm{outputBytes}).
\]
\end{enumerate}

For each evaluator $E_j$ and instance $i$, the garbler additionally commits evaluator-scoped OT transcript roots:
\[
\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}.
\]
These roots are later challengeable on opened instances via deterministic recomputation from revealed seed material.

The verifier-side seed is produced from evaluator commit-reveal inputs. Let
$s_j$ be the revealed seed of evaluator $E_j$ (or $0$ when defaulted by liveness
logic). The protocol derives
\[
\mathrm{verifierSeed}=\bigoplus_j s_j,
\]
and selects the evaluation instance deterministically as
\[
m = H(\texttt{"M"},\mathrm{verifierSeed},\mathrm{circuitId},\mathrm{contractAddress}) \bmod N.
\]
All non-selected instances form the opened set $S_{\mathrm{open}}$ for cut-and-choose verification.

\section{Phase-by-Phase Protocol Flow}
\subsection{Pre-Phase: Participant Set}
Before deposits start, the garbler registers additional evaluators (buyers in
the ENS-auction instantiation). This freezes the evaluator set for the run and
binds each participant to a public receiver address used in final assignment.

\subsection{Phase 1: Deposits}
The garbler and all registered evaluators lock collateral. If deposits are
incomplete at the deadline, refund paths allow participants to recover funds.
Successful completion advances the protocol to verifier-seed handling.

\subsection{Phase 2: Evaluator Seed Commit-Reveal and Evaluation Index}
Each evaluator first commits a hash of $(\mathrm{seed},\mathrm{salt})$, then
reveals $(\mathrm{seed},\mathrm{salt})$ in the reveal window. Missing commits or
reveals trigger default-to-zero seed with slashing to the garbler under current
contract rules. Once all evaluators are resolved (ready or defaulted), the
contract finalizes $\mathrm{verifierSeed}$ and computes $m$ deterministically.

This phase prevents one-party late selection of the evaluation index and ensures that cut-and-choose selection is reproducible from on-chain state.

\subsection{Phase 3: Garbler Commitments}
Phase 3 has two contract-enforced steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Core commitments:} the garbler submits $(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i)$ for all $i$.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT commitments:} for every evaluator $E_j$, the garbler submits $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ for all $i$.
\end{enumerate}
If the garbler misses these deadlines, evaluators can trigger an abort path that
reimburses evaluator collateral and transfers garbler collateral according to
the contract rules.

\subsection{Phase 4: Evaluator Liveness and Input Readiness}
Each evaluator must confirm readiness before deadline. Evaluators that remain
unresolved are defaulted to semantic input $0$ and slashed. This guarantees
progress to the opening phase even under partial non-cooperation.

\subsection{Phase 5: Cut-and-Choose Openings}
The garbler reveals $N-1$ seeds for indices in $S_{\mathrm{open}}$ and proves
consistency with $\mathrm{comSeed}_i$. Failure to reveal consistently or on time
leads to evaluator-triggerable penalty paths.

After valid openings, only ready evaluators remain active for dispute closure.
This is important in the $n$-buyer setting because defaulted participants do not
block dispute completion.

\subsection{Phase 6: Dispute Window}
During the dispute window, ready evaluators may open one of two explicit
challenge tracks:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GC leaf dispute:} challenge one gate leaf on an opened instance using
\begin{enumerate}
\item revealed instance seed,
\item claimed gate leaf bytes,
\item incremental-hash proof to $\mathrm{rootGC}_i$,
\item layout proof to the circuit layout commitment.
\end{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT root dispute:} for evaluator $E_j$ and opened instance $i$, recompute expected $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ from revealed seeds and domain-separated identifiers; mismatch slashes garbler, false challenge slashes evaluator.
\end{enumerate}

The protocol advances from dispute to label reveal when all active evaluators
close their dispute state, or when the dispute deadline expires.

\subsection{Phase 7: Label Reveal and Evaluation Payload Binding}
The garbler reveals garbler labels for the evaluation instance $m$. In the same
transition, the contract checks that \texttt{blobhash(0)} equals committed
$\mathrm{blobHashGC}_m$. This binds settlement to one concrete EIP-4844 payload
reference in that transaction (specific blob index and versioned hash), but it
is not a proof of payload correctness or full computation correctness: the EVM
cannot read blob contents and therefore cannot re-execute garbling on-chain.
Because blob data is ephemeral, this PoC assumes payload retrievability at
least throughout the dispute horizon (Assumption~A4)~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.

\subsection{Phase 8: Settlement and Assignment}
Any participant can submit \texttt{outputBytes}. The contract accepts only if:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $H(\texttt{"OUT"},\mathrm{circuitId},m,\mathrm{outputBytes}) = \mathrm{hOut}_m$,
\item decoded fields are well-formed and within bounds (winner id, winning bid, offered name hash).
\end{enumerate}

In the current proof-of-concept encoding:
\[
\mathrm{outputBytes}=\mathrm{encodePacked}(\mathrm{winnerId},\mathrm{winningBid},\mathrm{chosenNamehash}).
\]
After acceptance, the contract applies first-price transfer logic (winner pays own bid from collateral vault), records winner metadata, and transitions to final ENS assignment and payout settlement.
Therefore, accepted settlement in this implementation means anchor-consistent and well-formed output bytes, not on-chain re-verification of complete off-chain GC evaluation.

\section{n-Evaluator Generalization (Auction Instantiation: n Buyers)}
Relative to a single-evaluator baseline, the protocol generalizes by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item evaluator-scoped OT commitments $\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}$ instead of one global OT root,
\item per-evaluator liveness/default handling in Phase 4,
\item per-evaluator dispute closure tracking in Phase 6,
\item and deterministic $m$ derived from aggregated evaluator seed material.
\end{enumerate}
This keeps the protocol backbone unchanged while allowing one run to include
multiple competing evaluators (buyers in the auction application) under a
common commitment/dispute framework.

\section{Protocol vs Application Layer}
The protocol core is intentionally application-agnostic: commitments, openings, disputes, deadlines, and settlement anchors are independent of specific business rules. The ENS auction logic is the current application mapping:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the computed output determines winner identity and winning bid,
\item final assignment binds the chosen ENS name hash to the winner's registered receiver,
\item and first-price payment is executed from vault balances.
\end{enumerate}

The implementation currently models a fixed shortlist of offered ENS name hashes and validates that the chosen output name belongs to that offered set. This is an engineering simplification of the broader application vision and does not change the protocol-level enforcement structure.

\section{Specification Conformance}
For reproducibility, the protocol notation and ordering in this chapter follow
the implemented state machine.
In particular, output validity is specified via the structured anchor
$\mathrm{hOut}$ over packed output bytes, and the phase order places
verifier-seed finalization before the garbler's core commitment submission.
These choices keep the specification directly consistent with the contract-level
transition logic used in the prototype.

\section{On-Chain Architecture}
\subsection{State Machine and Core Objects}
The contract implementation in \texttt{MillionairesProblem.sol} realizes the protocol as an explicit finite state machine with hard deadlines.
The stage set includes deposits, evaluator seed commit/reveal, core
commitments, evaluator-scoped OT commitments, evaluator liveness finalization,
opening, dispute, label reveal, settlement, assignment, and closure.

Protocol-critical commitment objects are stored per cut-and-choose instance:
\[
(\mathrm{comSeed}_i,\mathrm{rootGC}_i,\mathrm{blobHashGC}_i,\mathrm{hOut}_i),
\]
and per evaluator-instance OT commitments:
\[
\mathrm{rootOT}_{j,i}.
\]
This state layout directly supports dispute traceability and deterministic verification of opened instances.

\subsection{Phase Handlers}
The contract exposes dedicated handlers for each transition class:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Seed handling and evaluation-index derivation:} evaluator seed commit/reveal finalization and deterministic $m$ selection.
\item \textbf{Commitment publication:} the garbler submits core commitments, then evaluator-scoped OT roots.
\item \textbf{Liveness progression:} unresolved evaluators are defaulted and slashed after deadline, allowing protocol progress.
\item \textbf{Opening and dispute entry:} the garbler reveals $N-1$ openings; active evaluators enter dispute window.
\item \textbf{Settlement and assignment:} output anchor validation, vault updates, winner recording, and final assignment.
\end{enumerate}

This implementation structure keeps transition logic auditable on-chain and avoids ambiguous cross-phase behavior.

\subsection{Dispute and Accountability Mechanism}
Two explicit dispute predicates are implemented:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{GC leaf dispute:} verifies one challenged gate leaf on an opened instance via seed-consistent recomputation, incremental-hash proof, and layout proof.
\item \textbf{Evaluator-scoped OT root dispute:} recomputes expected OT root for one evaluator and one opened instance; mismatch slashes garbler, false challenge slashes evaluator.
\end{enumerate}

The dispute window closes either when all active evaluators close their dispute
state or when deadline-based finalization is triggered.
This design provides bounded on-chain verification while preserving economically enforceable blame assignment.

\subsection{Settlement and Assignment Path}
Settlement accepts an output only if the computed anchor equals committed $\mathrm{hOut}_m$ for the evaluation instance.
The accepted output is decoded into winner id, winning bid, and chosen name hash.
The contract then applies first-price vault transfer logic and executes assignment in a separate finalization step.
This separation keeps settlement correctness checks explicit and makes final payout/assignment transitions observable on-chain.

\subsection{Smart-Contract Code Excerpts (Security-Critical Paths)}
Excerpts illustrate enforcement; the security argument is in Chapter~3/4.

To make implementation-level security properties explicit, this subsection shows
selected Solidity excerpts from \texttt{MillionairesProblem.sol}. The selected
fragments represent four complementary protection layers:
\begin{enumerate}
\item stage and role gating with timeout-driven liveness/slashing,
\item EIP-4844 blob binding for data-availability integrity,
\item bounded dispute verification with deterministic blame assignment,
\item output-anchor settlement binding plus domain bounds checks.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[style=soliditysnippet,caption={Stage/role gating and timeout-enforced liveness in seed commit phase.},label={lst:seed-commit-liveness}]
function commitBuyerSeed(bytes32 commitment) public {
    require(currentStage == Stage.BuyerSeedCommit, "Wrong stage");
    require(block.timestamp <= deadlines.verifierSeed, "Seed-commit deadline missed");
    require(isBuyer[msg.sender], "Only buyer");
    require(commitment != bytes32(0), "Empty buyer seed commitment");
    require(buyerSeedCommitment[msg.sender] == bytes32(0), "Buyer seed already committed");
    buyerSeedCommitment[msg.sender] = commitment;
}

function finalizeBuyerSeedCommitAfterDeadline() public {
    require(currentStage == Stage.BuyerSeedCommit, "Wrong stage");
    require(block.timestamp > deadlines.verifierSeed, "Seed-commit phase still active");
    require(_isParticipant(msg.sender), "Only participant");
    // Non-committed buyers are defaulted to seed=0 and slashed.
}
\end{lstlisting}

This path demonstrates explicit anti-stall logic: protocol progression is not
blocked by a non-responsive evaluator because timeout finalization can be
triggered permissionlessly by participants, with deterministic penalties for
non-cooperation.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=soliditysnippet,caption={Blob-hash commitment check before label reveal for instance $m$.},label={lst:blob-binding}]
function revealGarblerLabels(bytes32[] calldata _labels) external {
    require(currentStage == Stage.Labels, "Wrong stage");
    require(msg.sender == alice, "Only Garbler");
    require(block.timestamp <= deadlines.labels, "Label reveal deadline missed");
    require(_labels.length == bitWidth, "Bad labels length");

    bytes32 bHash = blobhash(0);
    require(bHash != bytes32(0), "Garbled Table Blob missing");
    require(bHash == instanceCommitments[m].blobHashGC, "Blob does not match Phase 2 commitment");

    garblerLabels = _labels;
}
\end{lstlisting}

This check binds on-chain progression to the exact evaluation payload hash and
prevents substitution of a different garbled-table blob after commitments.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=soliditysnippet,caption={Bounded GC-leaf dispute check with slashing outcome.},label={lst:gc-dispute}]
function challengeGateLeaf(
    uint256 instanceId,
    uint256 gateIndex,
    GateDesc calldata g,
    bytes calldata leafBytes,
    bytes32[] calldata ihProof,
    bytes32[] calldata layoutProof
) public {
    require(currentStage == Stage.Dispute, "Wrong stage");
    require(block.timestamp <= deadlines.dispute, "Dispute deadline missed");
    require(isBuyer[msg.sender], "Only buyer for dispute");
    require(buyerStatus[msg.sender] == BuyerStatus.Ready, "Buyer not active in dispute");

    _assertValidLayoutProof(gateIndex, g, layoutProof);
    bytes32 seed = _requireRevealedSeedForOpenedInstance(instanceId);
    bytes32 leafHash = _gateLeafHash(gateIndex, leafBytes);
    require(_processIncrementalProof(leafHash, ihProof) == instanceCommitments[instanceId].rootGC, "Bad IH proof");

    bytes memory expected = recomputeGateLeafBytes(seed, instanceId, gateIndex, g);
    bool matchLeaf = (_gateLeafHash(gateIndex, expected) == leafHash);
    if (!matchLeaf) _slashAliceToAllBuyers();
    else _slashBuyerToAlice(msg.sender);
}
\end{lstlisting}

The dispute path is intentionally leaf-bounded: the contract verifies one
challenge unit with compact proofs, then applies immediate economic resolution.
This keeps verification gas-bounded while preserving public accountability.

\begin{lstlisting}[style=soliditysnippet,caption={Settlement anchor binding and output-domain checks.},label={lst:settlement-binding}]
function settle(bytes calldata outputBytes) external {
    require(currentStage == Stage.Settle, "Wrong stage");
    require(_isParticipant(msg.sender), "Only participant");
    require(block.timestamp <= deadlines.settle, "Settlement deadline missed");
    require(_computeOutputAnchor(m, outputBytes) == instanceCommitments[m].hOut, "Invalid output commitment");

    (uint16 outWinnerId, uint64 outWinningBid, bytes32 outChosenNamehash) = _decodeOutput(outputBytes);
    require(outWinnerId < buyers.length, "winnerId out of bounds");
    require(uint256(outWinningBid) <= vault[buyers[outWinnerId]], "winningBid exceeds winner vault");
    require(
        outChosenNamehash == offeredNamehashes[0] ||
        outChosenNamehash == offeredNamehashes[1] ||
        outChosenNamehash == offeredNamehashes[2],
        "chosenNamehash not offered"
    );
}
\end{lstlisting}

This final gate enforces that only an output consistent with the committed
instance-$m$ anchor and auction-domain constraints can trigger value transfer
and assignment state transitions.

\subsection{Application Layer: ENS Auction}
In this proof-of-concept, the protocol is instantiated as an ENS auction application.
The application-specific mapping is implemented as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item bids are provided by buyers $B_1,\dots,B_n$, while the protocol computes and enforces the winner outcome,
\item the settlement payload encodes \texttt{winnerId}, \texttt{winningBid}, and \texttt{chosenNamehash},
\item the contract validates that the chosen name hash belongs to the offered ENS set and that the winning bid is economically valid,
\item final assignment is executed by calling the ENS adapter with the winner receiver address after settlement finalization.
\end{enumerate}
This makes the ENS auction an implemented application layer over the protocol core, not only an abstract example.

\section{Off-Chain Architecture}
\subsection{Shared Protocol Library}
The crate \texttt{off-chain-common} defines shared deterministic protocol logic
used by both garbler and evaluator services:
\begin{enumerate}
\item circuit and label derivation rules,
\item garbling/evaluation helpers,
\item incremental-hash and Merkle utilities for dispute evidence,
\item OT transcript reconstruction and root derivation,
\item settlement/output-anchor derivation,
\item canonical evaluation blob encoding and EIP-4844 versioned-hash derivation.
\end{enumerate}
Using one shared library is a protocol-integrity decision: both roles derive identical hashes, roots, and anchors from the same inputs.

\subsection{Garbler Off-Chain Service}
The \texttt{off-chain-alice} service performs garbler-side preparation and publication.
Its command set covers deposit, commitment construction, OT-root submission, opening reveal, label reveal, and evaluation artifact export.
In particular, garbler-side tooling produces the exact commitment tuple expected
by the contract and can emit evaluation payload artifacts for blob-linked
settlement flow.

\subsection{Evaluator Off-Chain Services}
The \texttt{off-chain-bob} service performs evaluator-side actions for each
evaluator $E_j$ (buyer $B_j$ in the ENS application):
\begin{enumerate}
\item seed commit/reveal and liveness signaling,
\item local evaluation for the selected instance,
\item dispute packet preparation (GC leaf track and OT-root track),
\item settlement submission and assignment finalization.
\end{enumerate}
This role separation supports the $n$-evaluator extension while preserving one
protocol logic core.

\section{Protocol-to-Code Mapping}
Implementation traceability is one of the core PoC goals.
The protocol phases in Chapter~4 map to concrete implementation entry points as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Deposits and participant readiness:} contract deposit and evaluator-ready paths plus evaluator-side commands.
\item \textbf{Verifier-seed commit/reveal:} evaluator commit/reveal handlers on-chain and corresponding evaluator commands off-chain.
\item \textbf{Core commitments and OT roots:} garbler commitment and OT-root submission handlers on-chain, commitment builders in garbler service.
\item \textbf{Opening/dispute:} reveal-openings, dispute handlers, and off-chain dispute packet generation.
\item \textbf{Settlement/assignment:} off-chain output-anchor derivation plus on-chain settlement and assignment handlers.
\end{enumerate}

The important point is that protocol claims are not narrative-only: each step is bound to executable code paths in Solidity and Rust.

\section{PoC Design Choices and Scope}
This thesis uses a proof-of-concept implementation to validate protocol enforceability end-to-end.
Several choices are intentional protocol-engineering decisions for this PoC and should not be interpreted as missing core work:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Shared deterministic GC/OT rules across Solidity and Rust:} the constrained canonical representation is required to guarantee verifier parity.
\item \textbf{Bounded on-chain verification:} leaf-level and root-level checks are a deliberate architecture choice for Ethereum feasibility, not an omitted full-verification feature.
\item \textbf{Hash-bound evaluation payload publication:} blob-hash commitment checks enforce data binding for settlement while keeping heavy data handling off the contract path~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\end{enumerate}

Accordingly, the PoC is not a ``toy simplification'' of the protocol objective.
It is the concrete implementation layer used to demonstrate that the protocol model is enforceable under Ethereum constraints.

\chapter{Evaluation}
\section{Evaluation Objectives}
The evaluation is implementation-driven and targets protocol properties that are critical for economically secure execution:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Correct settlement binding:} accepted outputs must match the committed evaluation-instance anchor and pass output decoding checks.
\item \textbf{Public accountability under deviation:} detectable deviations should trigger deterministic blame assignment and slashing.

\item \textbf{Application-level closure:} in the non-disputed path, protocol settlement must complete the ENS assignment mapping.
\end{enumerate}

This chapter therefore evaluates the implemented protocol behavior under representative end-to-end scenarios rather than only presenting abstract argumentation~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC,BitPriv2025}.

\section{Scenario Results}
\subsection{Scenario 1: Honest Success Flow}
The protocol completed all phases and reached \texttt{Closed} with successful assignment.
The accepted settlement decoded winner index $1$ (buyer $B_2$), winning bid $0.1$ ETH, and a valid offered ENS name hash (\texttt{tuvienna.eth} in this run).
The script checks confirmed:
\begin{enumerate}
\item anchor validity (\texttt{hOut} match),
\item winning-bid bound (\texttt{winningBid <= deposit}),
\item assignment success (\texttt{assigned=true}),
\item no unresolved evaluators at closure.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scenario 2: Evaluator Defaulted Flow}
One evaluator ($E_3$, buyer $B_3$ in the application mapping) was forced into
default in the evaluator-input phase.
The contract applied the intended liveness rule: the defaulted evaluator was
semantically mapped to zero input and slashed (collateral transfer to the
garbler), while remaining evaluators continued the run.
The protocol still completed settlement and assignment, finishing in \texttt{Closed} with \texttt{assigned=true}.
This run demonstrates that single-evaluator non-cooperation does not create
indefinite blocking under the implemented timeout/default path.

\subsection{Scenario 3: Garbler Cheat Flow}
The garbler was configured to publish a tampered \texttt{rootGC} for one opened
instance. An evaluator submitted a GC-leaf dispute, the mismatch was validated
by contract checks, and the garbler was slashed according to protocol rules.
The contract transitioned directly to \texttt{Closed} without accepting settlement output and without assignment (\texttt{assigned=false}), which is the intended safety behavior for a proven integrity violation.

\section{Requirement Coverage from the Results}
The executed scenarios provide direct evidence for key requirements defined in Chapter~3:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{R2 (Output correctness and binding):} Scenario 1 and Scenario 2 accepted settlement only with valid \texttt{hOut}-bound output bytes and bounded decoded fields.
\item \textbf{R3 (Publicly verifiable accountability):} Scenario 3 shows objective, contract-verifiable evidence leading to deterministic slashing of a cheating party.
\item \textbf{R4 (Liveness with penalties):} Scenario 2 demonstrates progress despite non-cooperative evaluator behavior via default and slashing.
\item \textbf{R5 (Settlement integrity):} successful scenarios complete deterministic first-price transfer and assignment in a protocol-consistent terminal state.
\end{enumerate}

Importantly, the settlement check scope in these runs is anchor equality plus
decoding constraints. It does not, by itself, prove on-chain that
\texttt{outputBytes} were produced by full GC evaluation; that assurance depends
on the off-chain evaluator/evidence model and the bounded dispute predicates.

Overall, the evaluation confirms that the Solidity/Rust prototype enforces the intended security-economics control points in executable runs, not only at specification level.

\section{Cost and Performance Discussion}
This chapter focuses on behavioral correctness and enforceability first.
The implementation follows bounded on-chain verification: contract logic validates commitments, anchors, and dispute predicates, while heavy garbled-circuit computation remains off-chain.
Evaluation-payload publication is hash-bound through EIP-4844 blob commitment checks, which keeps settlement binding explicit without full payload re-execution on-chain~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}. The \texttt{blobhash(0)} check binds to one concrete blob in the settlement transaction and supports availability/binding in the EIP-4844 window; it is not equivalent to permanent calldata storage and does not by itself certify computational correctness.

At the same time, full quantitative gas profiling (per-function gas table for optimistic and dispute paths) is intentionally separated as follow-up measurement work.
The current reported runs were executed on a local deterministic test chain, which is suitable for protocol validation and reproducibility, but not sufficient for final fee-economics conclusions under production network conditions.

\section{Threats to Validity}
The presented evidence is strong for implementation-level protocol behavior, but has known limits:
\begin{enumerate}
\item environment is local test-chain execution rather than live Ethereum conditions with volatile fees and ordering pressure,
\item scenario coverage is centered on the main representative strategic failure modes (honest, evaluator-default, garbler-cheat) for the evaluated protocol model,
\item evaluation scripts expose selected runtime values (including bids) for experiment transparency; privacy guarantees discussed in this thesis refer to protocol-level execution without such debug disclosure.
\end{enumerate}

These limits do not invalidate the demonstrated protocol properties; they delimit what is claimed in this chapter.

\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}
\section{Discussion of the Main Findings}
This thesis set out to design and validate an economically secure 2PC protocol for Ethereum with sealed-bid auctions as the application context.
The implementation and evaluation show that this objective is feasible when protocol enforcement is expressed as a strict on-chain state machine and expensive cryptographic work remains off-chain.

Three findings are central.
First, \textbf{output binding and settlement integrity} are enforceable through structured anchor checks (\texttt{hOut}) and deterministic decoding constraints.
Second, \textbf{accountability under deviation} can be operationalized with
bounded dispute predicates (GC-leaf and evaluator-scoped OT-root tracks) and
deterministic slashing outcomes.
Third, \textbf{liveness with strategic participants} can be preserved by deadline-driven default and penalty paths.

These findings align with the publicly verifiable financially secure 2PC direction in prior literature while making the enforcement model concrete for Ethereum execution constraints~\cite{Zhu2019PV2PC,BitPriv2025}.

\section{Protocol-Core vs Application Layer}
A key design choice in this work is to keep the protocol core independent from business logic.
Commitments, openings, disputes, and settlement anchors are protocol-layer mechanisms; the ENS auction is an application-layer instantiation over that backbone.
In the current PoC, this instantiation is realized by first-price settlement and deterministic assignment of the selected ENS name hash to the winner receiver.

This separation matters for generality.
It indicates that the same enforcement structure can support other private-input applications with different output semantics, provided they can be encoded into anchor-bound settlement bytes and deterministic payout rules.

\section{Limitations}
The presented contribution is intentionally bounded and has several limits:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Bounded on-chain verification:} this PoC provides economically enforceable execution with bounded dispute predicates; it does not provide on-chain verifiable computation of the full auction output.
\item \textbf{Evaluation environment:} reported runs use deterministic local-chain execution, which is suitable for correctness and reproducibility but not a final basis for production fee-economics claims.
\item \textbf{Application simplification:} the ENS mapping uses a fixed offered-name shortlist and deterministic decoding interface, prioritizing protocol validation over full auction-market design coverage.
\end{enumerate}

These limitations are consistent with the proof-of-concept scope and do not contradict the demonstrated enforcement properties.

\section{Future Work}
The next steps are clear and technically grounded:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Quantitative gas/cost profiling:} complete per-function cost tables for optimistic and dispute paths on realistic fee settings, including blob-related publishing costs~\cite{Buterin2022EIP4844}.
\item \textbf{Stronger economic analysis:} derive explicit collateral/deadline conditions for deviation deterrence under different attacker models and network conditions.
\item \textbf{Protocol scaling and robustness:} extend systematic testing for larger evaluator sets (buyers in concrete applications) and richer adversarial combinations while preserving deterministic dispute closure.
\item \textbf{Application expansion:} generalize from the current ENS PoC mapping toward richer auction policies while keeping protocol-core invariants unchanged.
\item \textbf{Alternative architecture comparison:} evaluate trust and verification tradeoffs of cross-layer/coprocessor approaches as secondary baselines, while preserving the L1-enforcement-first contribution of this thesis~\cite{Haloani2024COTIv2}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
This thesis contributes an implementation-backed protocol design for economically secure private computation on Ethereum.
The main result is not only conceptual: the Solidity/Rust prototype demonstrates that privacy-preserving off-chain computation, publicly verifiable on-chain enforcement, and deterministic settlement/assignment can be integrated into one coherent workflow.

In short, the work shows that economically secure 2PC on Ethereum is practically realizable with strict phase discipline, bounded verification, and explicit incentive-aligned fallback paths.
This forms a concrete foundation for subsequent production-hardening and broader private-market applications.
